## Your Voice Has More Range Than You're Using

A well-structured speech with a flat delivery is just an essay read out loud. The words matter, but how you say them is what makes people listen.

That's vocal variety: pitch, pace, pauses, and projection. Four tools. Most speakers lean on one or two and ignore the rest. Learning to use all four is what turns a speech people sit through into one they remember.

## Pitch: Where Your Voice Sits

Pitch is how high or low your voice sounds. Not how loud. Think piano keys, not volume knobs.

When your voice rises, it signals excitement, surprise, or a question. When it drops, it signals weight, seriousness, authority. Both are useful. Neither should be your default for an entire speech.

The problem most speakers have isn't a bad pitch. It's a stuck one. They find a comfortable range and stay there for six minutes. The audience stops hearing the words because the sound never changes.

In storytelling, pitch is how you move people through the emotional arc. A high pitch during the moment of surprise. A low pitch when the lesson lands. Your voice tells the audience how to feel before your words do.

## Pace: Fast Enough to Excite, Slow Enough to Land

Speed up when the energy calls for it. A breakthrough moment, a rapid sequence of events, building tension. Faster pace pulls people forward.

Slow down when the point matters. The key insight, the emotional turn, the sentence you want them to take home. Slower pace gives the audience time to absorb what you just said.

Most speakers do one or the other for the whole speech. The power is in the contrast. A fast section followed by a slow, deliberate sentence hits harder than either would alone.

## Pauses: The Most Underused Tool in Speaking

A pause before a key point builds anticipation. A pause after it lets the point sink in. A pause in the middle of a sentence creates tension.

New speakers are afraid of silence. They fill every gap with "um" or rush to the next sentence. But silence, held with confidence, communicates more than words. It says: what I'm about to say matters. Or: what I just said was worth sitting with.

## Projection: More Than Just Being Loud

Projection isn't volume for the sake of volume. It's making sure your voice reaches the back of the room without sounding like you're yelling.

Good projection comes from breathing. Deep, diaphragmatic breathing gives your voice power without strain. When you project well, you can also pull back. Drop to a near-whisper and the whole room leans in. That contrast only works if you have somewhere to drop from.

Think of projection as a dial, not a switch. You're adjusting constantly based on the moment, the room, and what the speech needs.

## Try This

Take one sentence: "Let me ask you a question. What is vocal variety?"

Now say it four different ways. Say the first part fast and loud, pause, then ask the question slowly and quietly. Then reverse it. Then try it deadpan. Then try it like you're genuinely curious.

Record all four. Listen back. You'll hear how much the meaning changes when the delivery changes, even though the words are identical.

## How to Get Better at This

Watch your speech recordings. Not for content. For sound. Are you varying your pitch or stuck in one range? Do you speed up and slow down, or does the whole speech run at one pace? Are you pausing, or are you filling? Are you projecting, or are you just loud?

Then pick one element and work on it in your next speech. Not all four at once. One. Pitch this time. Pace next time. Pauses the time after that. Stack the skills over several speeches and they'll start working together naturally.
